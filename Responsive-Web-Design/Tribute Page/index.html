The man who knew infinity

    About Srinivasa Ramanujan
  • Srinivasa Ramanujan (22 December 1887 – 26 April 1920) was an Indian mathematician who lived during the British Rule in India.
  • When Ramanujan was thirteen, he could work out S.L. Loney’s Trigonometry exercises without any help!
  • This self-taught mathematician received degree from Trinity College,Cambridge.
  • 22nd December is called National Mathematics Day in India because of Ramanujan's birth-anniversary!
  • After a funny incident, 1729 is called Hardy-Ramanujam number in his honor, and such numbers are called Taxicab numbers.
  • He became one of the youngest Fellow of Royal Society and the first Indian to be elected as Fellow of Trinity College,Cambridge.
  • During his short life, Ramanujan independently compiled nearly 3,900 results (mostly identities and equations.
  • Of his original letters, G.H. Hardy stated that a single look was enough to show they could only have been written by a mathematician of the highest calibre, comparing Ramanujan to mathematical geniuses such as Euler and Jacobi.
  • In 1910, after a meeting between the 23-year-old Ramanujan and the founder of the Indian Mathematical Society, V. Ramaswamy Aiyer, Ramanujan began to get recognition in Madras's mathematical circles, leading to his inclusion as a researcher at the University
  • Hardy came up with a scale of mathematical ability that went from 0 to 100. He put himself at 25. David Hilbert, the great German mathematician, was at 80. Ramanujan was 100.
  • When he died in 1920 at the age of 32, Ramanujan left behind three notebooks and a sheaf of papers (the “lost notebook”). These notebooks contained thousands of results that are still inspiring mathematical work decades later.
